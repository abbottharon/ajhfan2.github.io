The Quest for Resilient and Sustainable Agriculture

The sustainability of the agrifood sector is increasingly in the spotlight. Stakeholders ranging from consumers and shareholders to governments and businesses are showing a growing interest in the industry's sustainability performance. Concepts like regenerative agriculture and concerns about social licenses to operate on the path to Net Zero are gaining traction.

Australia has, over the past century, established itself as a global leader in sustainable agriculture. However, the sector must continue to innovate and find novel ways to support farmers and the supply chain in adapting to a changing climate. Quantifying sustainability performance is a challenge that demands new approaches to collect and analyse relevant data effectively.

Tools like FarmPrint, for instance, offer the ability to calculate a farm's greenhouse gas footprint and assess reductions resulting from changes in farming practices. Additionally, measuring farm animal welfare outcomes poses a significant scientific challenge, requiring the integration of data on animal behavior and environmental factors over the animals' lifetimes.

Traditionally steeped in tradition, agriculture is now embracing new technologies to enhance productivity and sustainability, and Melbourne is leading the charge.

Digital Agriculture: Revolutionizing Farming

Data and digital capabilities are revolutionizing every aspect of agriculture. Three decades ago, digital innovation in agriculture primarily revolved around simulation models of farming systems. These models allowed researchers to predict outcomes based on limited data. However, the landscape has dramatically evolved since then.

Today, digital applications in agriculture benefit from an abundance of data sources, including satellite data, weather records, detailed soil maps, and farm management software. This wealth of information enables industry stakeholders, from input suppliers to governments, to make informed decisions and drive greater efficiency.

A notable player in this field is Digital Agriculture Services, which leverages multiple data layers for every Australian farm, providing valuable insights to diverse customers for decision-making.

Moreover, digital technology is reshaping the way research is conducted, experiments are performed, and genetic improvements are made in plants and animals. This transformation is characterized by speed, automation, and informed decision-making. Digital decision tools, including improved climate forecasts, are pivotal in enabling agriculture to adapt to a changing and more variable climate.

Next Generation Biotech: Pioneering Sustainable Food Production

Biotechnology has already made significant inroads into agriculture through the development of genetically modified (GM) crops. While some promises remain unfulfilled, such as improving photosynthesis or nutrient uptake, GM crops like cotton and canola have delivered tangible benefits, including higher yields and reduced pesticide use.

The next frontier in agricultural biotechnology encompasses two innovative approaches: precision fermentation and gene editing. Precision fermentation allows for the manufacture of molecules, compounds, and products using synthetic organisms like yeasts, fuelled in part by agricultural biomass. This approach presents a sustainable alternative to traditional agriculture.

Gene editing, on the other hand, offers the precise engineering of crop and animal genomes. While regulatory and consumer acceptance remains a challenge, rigorous scientific testing is essential to ensure the safety and efficacy of these techniques.

Adding Value Across the Supply Chain

Australia has historically been known for producing and exporting raw commodities with little emphasis on value addition. Approximately 70% of produce, including grains and meat, is exported overseas as bulk commodities. However, the agribusiness sector is now poised to explore the seamless integration of production and processing science to create new value-adding opportunities.

Another promising avenue lies in harnessing agricultural waste, a significant proportion of which results from processing. Addressing this waste not only contributes to sustainability but also aligns with the principles of the circular economy, where one industry's waste becomes another's valuable input.

Companies like Nutri-V in Australia exemplify this approach by converting fresh vegetable waste into powders for use in various retail food products. Such initiatives not only reduce waste but also create high-value products from resources that would otherwise go to waste.
